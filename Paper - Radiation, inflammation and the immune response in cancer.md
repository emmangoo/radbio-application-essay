- "Only an estimated one-third of DNA damage is caused by the direct interaction of X-ray and γ-ray radiation hitting the macromolecule; the remaining two-thirds are due to indirect effects mediated by reactive oxygen/nitrogen species (ROS/RNS) generation (...). DNA damage includes DNA strand breaks, DNA–DNA crosslinks, DNA–protein crosslinks and modification of the deoxyribose rings and bases"
- Radiation received in fractions induces an accumulation of DNA breaks because not all breaks can be repaired after each dose 
- Ionising radiation through living tissues generates reactive oxygen/nitrogen species and several radicals and ions, all of which can damage cellular macromolecules 
- Ionising radiation can change the normal functions of the ER, mitochondria, and inflammosomes 
- Apoptosis "is induced by radiation via intrinsic mechanisms—DNA damage sensors, cytokines, stress response, DAMPs, ceramide and ROS/RNS initiated signalling pathways discussed elsewhere, or extrinsic mechanisms—up-regulation of death receptors"
- pro-inflammatory cytokines induce cell death, as a connected network and in conjunction with ROS & ligands => a lot of factors work together in response to radiation, in form of cascades and networks
- exposure to 137Cs radiation caused insufficient activation of protein C and thus coagulopathy
- "Radiation can alter the immunogenicity of tumours and increase their susceptibility to immune recognition and clearance. Upon irradiation, tumour cells and tumour-associated endothelial cells express a wide range of ‘novel’ antigens (called ‘neoantigens’) including immune regulatory molecules"
- Inflammosomes are also part of stress pathways and can cause apoptosis or pyroptosis upon activation
- Conclusion for the effect of radiation on the immune response: it depends on "radiation type, dose, DR, intensity/fractionation, delivery method, field size, and total cumulative dose"
