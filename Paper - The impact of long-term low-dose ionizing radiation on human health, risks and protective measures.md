- "Ionizing radiation, primarily derived from X-rays and gamma rays, can strip electrons from atoms or molecules, thereby damaging cellular and tissue structures"
- "The United Nations Scientific Committee on the Effects of Atomic Radiation (UNSCEAR) defines LDIR as an effective dose of ≤ 100.00 mSv, with corresponding organ absorbed doses for X/_γ_ rays typically below 100 mGy and a low dose rate of < 6 mSv/year"
- You can categorise the effects of LDIR either by effect size or probability of occurrence, the former needing a threshold 
- It is not certain that you can always design radiation protection protocols by the stochastic assumption that there is a linear relationship between radiation exposure and disease risk 
- Radiation hormesis: theory/ hypothesis that low doses of IR can be beneficial by activating repair mechanisms, but not sufficient epidemiological evidence
- it can be helpful for novel drug targets or radiation protection protocols to identify the "priming dose"
- Intergenerational / long time exposure to low-dose radiation can enhance genomic stability, potentially by inducing adaptive responses in eg, the TP53 pathway
- long term low-dose IR can lead to impaired hematopoiesis
- The thymus is particularly sensitive to IR => T and B lymphocytes are activated in the spleen, which is also sensitive to IR
- Lower CD4+ lymphocytes and IgG, IgA, IgM found in radiology workers exposed to low levels of IR
- long term low dose IR can hinder the maturation of dendritic cells => as DCs are the ones that present the antigen to T cells, this disrupts the start of the adaptive immune response 
- low dose IR can enhance T cell function and help them infiltrate a tumour 
- radiation can interfere with the immune memory formation
- "LDIR often affects the immune system through the production of free radicals and reactive oxygen species (ROS), and oxidative stress may cause damage to immune cells, altering their function and thereby affecting the immune response"
